Flow Matching, Briefly

Flow matching has become a convenient alternative to score-based diffusion for building generative models, including the flow-matching neural operators I use for multi-fidelity PDE surrogates. This post is a short, self-contained derivation of the core idea: a way to train a continuous normalizing flow without simulating it during training.

The probability flow ODE

We want to transport samples from a simple base density \( p_0 \) (e.g. a standard Gaussian) to a target data density \( p_1 \). Consider a time-dependent vector field \( v_t(x) \) for \( t \in [0, 1] \) that generates a flow \( \phi_t \) via the ordinary differential equation

\[ \frac{d}{dt}\phi_t(x) = v_t\big(\phi_t(x)\big), \qquad \phi_0(x) = x. \]

If \( x_0 \sim p_0 \) and we push it forward through \( \phi_t \), the resulting density \( p_t \) obeys the continuity equation

\[ \frac{\partial p_t}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \big(p_t v_t\big) = 0. \]

Any \( v_t \) satisfying this equation with \( p_1 \) matching the data distribution at \( t = 1 \) is a valid generative model. The trouble is that, in general, we do not know \( v_t \) in closed form.

Conditional flow matching

The key trick, due to Lipman et al. (2023), is to define a simple conditional path between an individual data point \( x_1 \) and noise, then show that regressing onto the conditional vector field recovers the correct marginal vector field in expectation. A common choice is the linear (optimal-transport) path

\[ x_t = (1 - t)\, x_0 + t\, x_1, \qquad x_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I), \]

whose conditional velocity is simply \( \dot{x}_t = x_1 - x_0 \). The training objective is then

\[ \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{t,\, x_0,\, x_1} \Big[\, \big\| v_\theta(x_t, t) - (x_1 - x_0) \big\|^2 \,\Big], \]

a plain regression loss — no simulation of the ODE and no score matching are required during training. At sample time, integrating \( v_\theta \) from \( t=0 \) to \( t=1 \) with an off-the-shelf ODE solver pushes noise samples to the data distribution.

Why this is useful for PDE surrogates

Replacing \( x \) with a discretized PDE field (or a fidelity-residual between a low- and high-fidelity solution) turns the same machinery into a resolution-invariant generative model for physical fields. The conditional objective above is what makes training tractable at the resolutions we care about, since it avoids backpropagating through an ODE solver. 

# minimal conditional flow-matching loss, PyTorch-style
def cfm_loss(v_theta, x0, x1):
    t = torch.rand(x0.shape[0], device=x0.device)
    t_ = t.view(-1, *([1] * (x0.dim() - 1)))
    xt = (1 - t_) * x0 + t_ * x1
    target = x1 - x0
    return ((v_theta(xt, t) - target) ** 2).mean()
